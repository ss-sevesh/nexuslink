## Hypothesis Statement

If the hierarchical clustering methods (cs.LG) in machine learning can be applied to protein structures (q-bio.BM), then a protein's similarity metric based on its secondary structure would correlate with its thermodynamic stability by a factor of at least 0.8.

## Evidence From

- [[Machine Learning]] ↔ [[Deep Learning]]

## Suggested Experiments

1. Design and implement a machine learning pipeline to map protein structures onto hierarchical clustering dendrograms, and then correlate the resulting similarity metric with experimentally measured thermodynamic stability (e.g., via differential scanning calorimetry).
2. Perform molecular dynamics simulations to calculate the free energy landscapes of protein variants with altered secondary structure; compare these energies to those predicted by machine learning models trained on structural hierarchies.
3. Use a range of techniques (e.g., circular dichroism spectroscopy, small-angle X-ray scattering) to determine protein secondary and tertiary structures after exposing them to thermal or chemical denaturation; use these results as experimental validation for the correlation between hierarchical clustering similarity and thermodynamic stability.
